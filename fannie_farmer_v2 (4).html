Fannie Merritt Farmer

Fannie Merritt Farmer (1857–1915) transformed American domestic cookery. After suffering a paralytic stroke at eighteen, she turned to cooking and enrolled at the Boston Cooking School, where she eventually became principal. Her 1896 cookbook — revised and expanded through multiple editions — was the first to standardize level measurements, replacing the vague “a handful” and “a wine-glass” with precise cups, tablespoons, and teaspoons.

The Book

The Boston Cooking-School Cook Book went through thirteen editions in Farmer’s lifetime. This archive draws from the 1918 revised edition — the most widely circulated version — comprising 1,409 recipes organized across 21 categories. Her clear, methodical prose set the template for American recipe writing throughout the twentieth century.

The Archive

This digital edition presents 1,409 recipes from the complete 1918 text, each linked directly to its page in the original book. Recipes include estimated portions and cooking times extracted from the original instructions, and a period-appropriate menu generator structured after Farmer’s own guidance on suitable dinner combinations.
